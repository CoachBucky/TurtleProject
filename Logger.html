Loggerhead Sea Turtle

Caretta caretta

Federally Listed as Threatened & Endangered

About the Loggerhead

Loggerheads are named for their massive heads and powerful jaws, which they use to crush the shells of their prey. They are the most common sea turtle that nests in Alabama.

  • Size: Adults can weigh up to 350 pounds and have a carapace (shell) length of about 3 feet.
  • Appearance: Their shell is heart-shaped and typically reddish-brown, while their skin is yellowish-brown.
  • Lifespan: They can live for more than 50 years.
  • Diet: They are primarily carnivores, feeding on bottom-dwelling invertebrates like crabs, conchs, and whelks.

Habitat & Alabama Range

Loggerheads are found in temperate and tropical regions of the Atlantic, Pacific, and Indian Oceans. They prefer coastal habitats like bays and estuaries, but also venture into the open ocean.

In Alabama:

They are found in the waters of the Gulf of Mexico. Nesting activity is crucial and primarily occurs on the beaches of Baldwin and Mobile counties, particularly along the Fort Morgan Peninsula and on Dauphin Island.

Nesting Habits

Females migrate long distances to return to the same beaches where they were born to lay their own eggs. They emerge from the water at night to dig their nests.

  • Nesting Season (AL): May through August.
  • Clutch Size: Typically 100-120 eggs per nest. A female may lay multiple nests in one season.
  • Incubation: Eggs incubate in the warm sand for about 60 days. The temperature of the sand determines the sex of the hatchlings.

A hatchling's perilous journey to the sea.

Movement & Locomotion

Loggerheads are powerful swimmers, using their long front flippers for propulsion and their shorter back flippers for steering. On land, they move much more clumsily.

  • In Water: They can swim at speeds of up to 15 mph in short bursts.
  • On Land: They use an "alternating gait," moving their front flippers one at a time, similar to walking. This is different from other sea turtles (like Green turtles) that pull with both front flippers at once.
  • Migration: They undertake long migrations between feeding grounds and nesting beaches.

Footprints for Tracking Models

Identifying turtle tracks is key to conservation efforts. The tracks indicate a nesting attempt and help volunteers locate and protect nests. Loggerhead tracks have a distinct pattern.

Key Characteristics:

  • Gait: Alternating (diagonal) marks from the front flippers. The tracks are not parallel.
  • Tail Drag: A wavy or straight line down the middle, created by the tail dragging in the sand.
  • No Rear Marks: Unlike freshwater turtles, their rear flippers don't usually leave distinct marks.
  • Width: The crawl is typically 25-35 inches wide.

Alabama Conservation Efforts

A key organization in Alabama is Share the Beach. They are a volunteer-run group dedicated to monitoring nests, protecting hatchlings, and educating the public along Alabama's coastline.
